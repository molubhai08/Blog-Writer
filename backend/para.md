# Human-like Writing Guidelines

## Key Rules

- Use short to medium length sentences (mostly 10-18 words). Avoid very long, complex sentences.
- Break ideas into smaller sentences instead of packing everything in one.
- Use simple connecting words like "as a result", "on the other hand", "in contrast", "however", "in this way", "at the same time".
- Write in a natural flow — like a knowledgeable teacher explaining to a student.
- Keep formal tone but avoid sounding robotic or overly polished.
- Use active voice where possible. Mix with passive when needed.
- Include specific examples (states, regions, rivers) but don't make it sound like a list.
- Aim for 120-160 words per paragraph unless specified.
- Do not use repetitive sentence structures.

## Few-Shot Examples

### Example 1: Regional Variations in Climate Change Impacts

Climate change affects different parts of India in different ways. The Himalayan region is seeing fast glacier melt. This threatens water supply in the northern plains. The Indo-Gangetic plains often face extreme floods and heat waves. In contrast, the western and central regions suffer from frequent droughts. States like Rajasthan and Maharashtra are badly hit. Coastal areas such as Odisha, Andhra Pradesh and the Sundarbans face rising sea levels and stronger cyclones. These events cause erosion and loss of mangroves. The Western Ghats and Northeast India are losing biodiversity due to changing rainfall and temperature. The Thar Desert is expanding because of desertification. These regional differences make it difficult to manage climate change uniformly across the country.

### Example 2: Mitigation and Adaptation Strategies for Climate Change in India

India is taking several steps to fight climate change. On the mitigation side, the country is expanding renewable energy like solar and wind power. It has set targets to reduce carbon emissions. The National Action Plan on Climate Change guides many programmes. For adaptation, India is building better disaster management systems. It promotes climate-resilient crops in agriculture. Coastal areas are getting mangrove restoration projects. Watershed management is helping fight droughts. The government is also running schemes like the National Adaptation Fund. These efforts combine both prevention and adjustment measures. However, more focus is needed on local-level implementation to make them truly effective.

### Example 3: Environmental Impact (UPSC-friendly)

Climate change is causing major damage to India's environment. Himalayan glaciers are melting quickly because of rising temperatures. This is affecting the flow of rivers like the Ganges and Brahmaputra. As a result, water availability may decline in the coming years. Monsoon rains have also become more uncertain. Some areas face heavy floods while others suffer long droughts. States like Bihar and Assam often see floods. On the other hand, Rajasthan and Maharashtra experience severe dry spells. These changes are leading to soil erosion and lower crop yields. Along the coast, sea levels are rising. Stronger cyclones are damaging mangroves in the Sundarbans. Biodiversity in the Western Ghats is also under threat due to habitat changes. In this way, climate change is weakening India's ecological balance.
